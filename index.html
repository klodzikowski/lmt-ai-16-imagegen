Variational Autoencoder (VAE) Foundation

Introduced by Kingma & Welling (2013). A VAE learns to compress images into a tiny latent space (a compact, hidden representation — think of it as a "summary" of the image in just a few numbers), then reconstruct them. Instead of memorising images pixel-by-pixel, it learns a smooth, continuous space where similar images are nearby. This is the foundation for almost all modern generative models.

Key insight: The VAE doesn't store images — it learns a compressed representation. The latent space is continuous: you can smoothly interpolate between a "1" and a "6" and get plausible digits in between. This is why VAEs (and their latent spaces) are the backbone of Stable Diffusion, DALL-E, and other modern generators — diffusion happens in the latent space, not on raw pixels.

How does a VAE work?

  • Encoder: Compresses a 28×28 image (784 pixels) down to just 2 numbers (the latent code z). Crucially, it outputs a range of possible codes, not a single fixed point.
  • Decoder: Takes those 2 numbers and reconstructs the full 28×28 image.
  • Loss = Reconstruction + Smoothness. The reconstruction part ensures the output looks like the input. The smoothness part ensures the latent space is well-organised — nearby points decode to similar images, with no "holes" or jumps.

Why MNIST?

MNIST (handwritten digits) is the classic VAE example because the data is simple enough that a 2D latent space can capture it. You can literally see the latent space: digits cluster by identity, and the space between clusters produces smooth transitions. With real images, the same principle applies — but the latent space has 4–256 dimensions instead of 2.

Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) Pioneering

Introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014). Two neural networks compete: a Generator (forger) tries to create convincing fake images, while a Discriminator (detective) tries to tell real from fake. They improve each other through this adversarial game. Yann LeCun called GANs "the most interesting idea in the last 10 years in ML."

Training is a two-player game: Think of the early Generator as the famously botched Ecce Homo restoration — it tries to recreate the original but produces something hilariously wrong. The Discriminator easily spots the fake. But round after round, the Generator improves its forgeries while the Discriminator gets better at detecting them. Eventually the Generator produces images so realistic that even the Discriminator can't tell. Then: discard the Discriminator, ship the Generator.
Inference is simple: Sample a random point from latent space, pass it through the trained Generator (a transposed CNN / decoder), and out comes a new image. No discriminator needed — it was only used during training. This is why GANs are fast at inference time.

How GAN training works

  1. Discriminator step: Feed the Discriminator a mix of real images and Generator fakes. It learns to classify them (real vs fake) using cross-entropy (a standard classification loss). Backprop updates only the Discriminator's weights.
  2. Generator step: The Generator creates fakes and labels them as real. The Discriminator evaluates them. The cross-entropy loss is backpropagated through the frozen Discriminator to update only the Generator's weights — teaching it to make more convincing fakes.
  3. Repeat: Alternate these two steps. The Generator gets better at fooling; the Discriminator gets better at detecting. This adversarial dynamic drives both networks to improve.
  4. Ship it: Once training converges, discard the Discriminator. The Generator alone is the final model.

Why GANs matter (and their limitations)

  • Produced the first photorealistic AI faces (StyleGAN, thispersondoesnotexist.com)
  • Mode collapse: Generator may learn to produce only a few types of images, ignoring diversity
  • Training instability: The adversarial game is hard to balance — one network can overpower the other
  • Largely superseded by diffusion models (2020+) which are more stable and diverse

Diffusion: Learning to Denoise Most Mature

Introduced by Ho et al. (2020). A diffusion model starts from pure Gaussian noise and iteratively removes it, guided by the text prompt. Modern models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) do this in a compressed latent space (from a VAE), not on raw pixels — making it much faster. Models: Stable Diffusion, DALL-E 2/3, Imagen, FLUX.

How diffusion works — step by step

  1. Compress to latent space: A pre-trained VAE encoder compresses the image (e.g. 1024×1024 → 128×128 latent). All diffusion happens in this small latent space — much faster than working with raw pixels.
  2. Forward process (training): Gradually add Gaussian noise to the latent over T steps until it becomes pure noise. This creates (latent, noise-level) training pairs.
  3. Neural network learns to denoise: A U-Net or Transformer is trained to predict the noise ε present at each step, conditioned on the text prompt (via cross-attention with CLIP/T5 text embeddings).
  4. Reverse process (generation): Start from pure noise in latent space. At each step t, the network predicts ε, which is subtracted to get a slightly cleaner latent. Repeat T times.
  5. Decode to pixels: The clean latent is passed through the VAE decoder to produce the final full-resolution image.
  6. Classifier-free guidance: Run the network twice — once with the prompt, once without — and amplify the difference. Higher guidance = stricter prompt adherence but less diversity.
Key insight: The model doesn't "draw" — it sculpts noise into an image, working in the VAE's compressed latent space (not raw pixels — that's why it's called Latent Diffusion). Early steps establish global layout and colour palette; later steps refine textures and fine detail. Modern models like FLUX use Rectified Flow (straight-line paths instead of curved) which allows just 1–4 steps via distillation.

Autoregressive: Treating Images as Language Key building block

First: tokenise the image into discrete codes using a VQ-VAE (a variant of the VAE from Tab 1, but with a fixed codebook of discrete tokens instead of continuous values). Then: predict tokens sequentially with a transformer, just like predicting words. The key question is in what order.

Raster scan (DALL-E 1, 2021): Tokens are predicted one by one, left-to-right, top-to-bottom — like typing a letter. 1,024 sequential steps for a 32×32 token grid. Slow, and the model can't "see" what's below or to the right, so spatial coherence is poor. This approach is obsolete.
Next-Scale / VAR (Tian et al., NeurIPS 2024 Best Paper): Instead of raster order, generate coarse-to-fine: 1×1 → 2×2 → 4×4 → … → full resolution. At each scale, all tokens are generated in parallel. Only ~10 autoregressive steps total. 20× faster than diffusion with state-of-the-art image quality, and it keeps improving predictably as you scale up the model.

How it works

  1. Tokenisation (VQ-VAE): A pre-trained encoder maps each image patch to the nearest entry in a learned codebook of ~8,192 discrete tokens. The image becomes a grid of integer IDs.
  2. Transformer prediction: A GPT-style transformer predicts the next token(s), conditioned on class label or text prompt. The key innovation is what "next" means.
  3. Decoding: Token IDs are mapped back to patches via the VQ-VAE decoder, producing the final image.

Hybrid / Transfusion Dominant approach (2025+)

Introduced by Meta (2024). A single transformer handles both text and images with two objectives: cross-entropy (next-word prediction) for text tokens, MSE diffusion loss (how close is the predicted noise to the actual noise?) for image patches. This is believed to be the architecture behind GPT-4o image generation, Google's Gemini image models, and Apple's on-device image generation in Apple Intelligence.

How Transfusion works

  1. Unified sequence: Text tokens and image patches live in the same sequence. Special BOI (Begin of Image) and EOI (End of Image) markers signal where images start and end.
  2. Dual training objective: Text tokens use standard cross-entropy (next-token prediction). Image patches use diffusion MSE loss (predict the noise). Same transformer, two losses.
  3. Text → sequential, Image → parallel: The model reads your prompt word-by-word (building up context), then when it emits BOI it switches to parallel diffusion mode — all image patches are denoised simultaneously over a few steps, guided by the prompt context.
  4. Back to text: After EOI, the model can continue generating text autoregressively (e.g., “Here’s your image!”).
Why this is the dominant approach: One model, one set of weights, understands both language and vision natively. The same transformer backbone handles text and images — no separate generation pipeline needed. The image appears all at once (parallel diffusion on latent patches), not token-by-token. This is why GPT-4o, Gemini, and Apple Intelligence can seamlessly mix text and images in a single conversation.

The key takeaway: None of these methods work on raw pixels — they all operate in a compressed latent/token space (from a VAE or VQ-VAE) and decode to pixels at the end. "Autoregressive image generation" does not mean the image appears token by token like a typewriter. Only the obsolete raster-scan approach (DALL-E 1, which was autoregressive — not diffusion) worked that way. Modern AR models generate coarse-to-fine (VAR) or use diffusion for the image patches (Transfusion/GPT-4o).
